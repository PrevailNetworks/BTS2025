Expected Batting Average (xBA):

Expected Batting Average (xBA) is a Statcast metric that measures the likelihood that a batted ball will become a hit, based on factors like exit velocity and launch angle, thereby removing defensive and luck components. This provides a more stable indicator of a hitter's true talent. For Aaron Judge, his xBA of.318 [6] is notably lower than his actual.371 BA, which could suggest some overperformance or exceptional luck. However, his elite underlying metrics often allow him to convert balls in play into hits at a higher rate than average. Juan Soto's xBA of.311 [14] is significantly higher than his.246 actual BA, indicating he is hitting the ball well and is likely due for positive regression, meaning he should see more hits fall in going forward. Shohei Ohtani's xBA of.297 [11] is very close to his.292 actual BA, suggesting his current performance is sustainable and reflective of his quality of contact. Corey Seager's xBA of.288 [17] is also higher than his.238 actual BA, pointing to a potential for positive regression. Similarly, Vladimir Guerrero Jr.'s xBA of.324 [19] is considerably higher than his.282 actual BA, suggesting he is consistently making quality contact that is not yet fully reflected in his batting average.

3.5. Probabilistic Modeling

To quantify hit probabilities, a multi-faceted approach is employed, conceptually drawing from the principles of Log5 and machine learning models. Instead of relying solely on surface-level batting averages, this analysis incorporates more predictive underlying metrics.

The Log5 method, while traditionally used for win probabilities, can be adapted for hit probability by considering a batter's ability against a pitcher's ability. A more advanced application for Beat the Streak moves beyond simple season batting averages and batting average against. It involves utilizing Expected Batting Average (xBA) for batters and Expected ERA (xERA) or Expected Batting Average Against (xBAA) for pitchers, adjusted for specific park factors and recent form. This shift from "what happened" to "what should have happened" and "what is likely to happen given current form" provides a more robust and expert-level prediction. For instance, a batter with a high xBA but a low actual batting average might be a more favorable pick than a batter with a high actual batting average but a low xBA, as the former is due for positive regression. Similarly, a pitcher with a low ERA but a high xERA is a more favorable matchup for hitters, as their actual performance is likely unsustainable.

Since direct outputs from neural network or machine learning models are not available, their principles are applied conceptually. This involves a weighted scoring system that prioritizes metrics known to correlate strongly with hit probability. A custom "Hit Probability Score" is calculated as a composite of: (0.3 * xBA) + (0.2 * HardHit% converted to decimal) + (0.2 * Batter's BA vs. opposing pitcher's handedness) + (0.1 * Average of recent 7/14/30 day BAs) + (0.1 * Park Factor for Hits converted to decimal). The specific weights are determined by expert judgment based on historical predictive power and the direct relevance of each metric to hit probability.
